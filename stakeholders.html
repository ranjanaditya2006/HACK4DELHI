Global comparative context
Lessons from Sweden, South Africa, and the Philippines that informed India’s proposed hybrid ONOE model.
Sweden – fully synchronized cycle
Elections for the Riksdag, Regional Councils, and Municipal Councils are held together every four years on the second Sunday of September, using three colour‑coded ballots. Snap elections do not reset the four‑year clock: any mid‑term house only serves the remainder of the original term.
India adopts this “unexpired term” rule in its ONOE proposal so that even if a government falls early, the synchronized national–state cycle remains intact.
South Africa – national + provinces together
National Assembly and all nine Provincial Legislatures vote together every five years using two ballots per voter, while municipal elections are held separately roughly mid‑term, giving local contests their own electoral space.
India’s two‑step plan (first align Lok Sabha and State Assemblies, then conduct municipal and panchayat polls within 100 days) sits midway between Sweden’s full integration and South Africa’s tier separation.
Philippines – mega synchronized elections
National and most local offices are elected together every three years on a single long ballot on the second Monday of May, while the President and Vice‑President are chosen in a separate six‑year cycle. This cuts election frequency but creates very large, high‑stakes contests.
India deliberately avoids this “all offices, one ballot” design, keeping a five‑year national–state cycle and only time‑bound alignment of local bodies to control ballot length and cost spikes.
India’s ONOE proposal is closest to a Sweden‑style fixed cycle, moderated by South Africa‑type tier staggering and a conscious decision not to replicate the Philippines’ mega‑election model that would put maximum strain on regional parties and lower‑capacity states.
